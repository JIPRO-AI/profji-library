## More Output Doesn't Mean More Creativity

Generative AI writes text, creates images, and writes code. Has creativity therefore risen automatically? No. Even if AI pours out a hundred variations, if humans cannot judge what to choose and why, the output becomes noise.

This article makes one core argument. **The key to AI raising creativity is not more generation, but humans designing a better selection structure.** Here, selection structure refers to the cycle of setting intent, evaluating value, verifying, and redirecting. When this structure operates, AI becomes a tool of cognitive amplification; when this structure is absent, AI becomes a trap of cognitive delegation.

This distinction originates from a measurement framework proposed by Eduardo Di Santi [1]. Di Santi offers a conceptual framework for distinguishing whether a person's cognitive capacity expands (amplification) or is offloaded externally (delegation) when interacting with AI. This framework has not yet undergone large-scale empirical validation, but it provides the most useful starting point for designing human-AI creative collaboration.

## 1. Extended Creativity: A Collaborative Structure, Not a Tool

Andrea Gaggioli and colleagues organize the human-AI relationship into three stages through the conceptual framework of "Extended Creativity": support, synergy, and symbiosis [2]. The core insight of this framework is that for AI to raise creativity, it is not enough to simply "generate more"—the very structure of the relationship with humans must change.

At the support stage, AI performs tasks such as retrieving material, generating drafts, and proposing variations. At the synergy stage, human input and AI output interact repeatedly, refining ideas. At the symbiosis stage, the boundary between human and AI roles becomes fluid, and the combination of the two produces results neither could reach alone.

What matters here is that as the stages advance, the human role does not shrink but **changes**. At the support stage, the human decides "what to ask for." At the synergy stage, the human judges "how to interpret this output and redirect it." At the symbiosis stage, the human evaluates "whether my intent and values are reflected in this result." At every stage, the human's cognitive contribution rises to a higher order.

What happens if this structure is ignored? Throwing "write me 20 marketing copy variations" at AI and using one of them as-is doesn't even reach the support stage. This is because the human never set an intent, never evaluated the output, and never redirected it. This is precisely the cognitive delegation that Di Santi warns against [1].

## 2. The Surprise Effect: The Moment AI Actually Changes Human Thinking

This article will call the most interesting psychological phenomenon in human-AI collaboration the "surprise effect." This is not a term settled in academic literature but a name this article assigns for explanatory convenience. When AI produces output a human did not expect, a crack forms in their existing cognitive structure. This crack becomes the catalyst that opens a new path of thought.

Concretely, it works like this. A designer requests a "minimalist brand logo," but AI produces a draft combining traditional African patterns with Bauhaus geometry. The designer is startled. But within that startle, a cognitive restructuring occurs: "Why did it produce this?" → "This combination is possible?" → "Was I defining minimalism too narrowly?" This is the surprise effect.

This effect matters because it raises cognitive flexibility. Cognitive flexibility is the ability to switch thinking strategies to fit a new situation. Humans have a strong tendency to rely on past experience and familiar patterns. AI's unpredictable output forcibly breaks this pattern dependence.

However, there is a condition for the surprise effect to translate into creativity. The human must **go through a process of interpreting and judging** that shock. If AI's unexpected output is accepted as-is, that isn't the surprise effect—it's uncritical acceptance. Conversely, dismissing it immediately loses the learning opportunity. The key is to pause for three seconds and think "why did it produce this?" This three-second pause is the fork in the road between cognitive amplification and cognitive delegation.

## 3. The Trap of Cognitive Delegation: When Convenience Erodes Capability

The more powerful AI becomes, the easier it is for humans to fall into two traps.

**Anchoring effect**: This is the phenomenon where the first answer AI presents becomes the reference point for all subsequent judgments. After a marketing planner sees AI's first suggestion, they end up evaluating the remaining options only by "how different is this from the first one." Chaeyeon Lim proposes a design direction for metacognition-based intervention strategies to mitigate this bias [3]. The core idea is to build a structure where the user first forms their own hypothesis before receiving AI output, then compares it against the AI output.

**Automation bias**: This is the tendency to automatically trust AI's answers. The moment "because AI said so" becomes the basis for judgment, human critical thinking capacity begins to degrade. Katelyn Xiaoying Mei and Nic Weber distinguish two design directions by which AI augments critical thinking [4]. One supplements thinking the human has already performed (Performed); the other grows the human's thinking capacity itself by showing examples of good thinking (Demonstrated). This distinction remains at the level of design principle, but it points to an important direction: when building AI systems, they should be designed not as "tools that give answers" but as "partners that demonstrate how to think."

Yun Dai emphasizes that student agency is a core variable of creativity in AI-assisted learning environments [5]. Here, agency is not mere control but the capacity to receive external stimuli, interpret them for oneself, and assign meaning. When this agency is maintained during collaboration with AI, cognitive amplification occurs; when agency disappears, it degenerates into cognitive delegation.

In sum, the core risk of cognitive delegation is the gradual erosion of capability. A single act of delegation is convenient. But if repeated, judgment ability in that domain weakens like "muscle loss" [1]. This is precisely why deliberate friction must be designed into practical workflows.

## 4. Practical Workflow: Turning an Abstract Frame into an Operating Model

That covers the theory. Now, how is this actually applied?

### 4-1. Role Division Matrix

| Stage | Human's Role | AI's Role |
|------|------------|----------|
| Setting Intent | Define goals, constraints, success criteria | — |
| Exploration | — | Generate diverse variations (at least 5) |
| Evaluation | Check bias, judge value, review contextual fit | — |
| Redirection | Revise direction, add new constraints | Regenerate reflecting revised conditions |
| Final Decision | Choose + take responsibility | — |

The key point of this matrix is that **there is no stage AI completes alone**. Even at the exploration stage, the human first specifies "along which axis to secure diversity."

### 4-2. A Concrete Workflow Example: Marketing Campaign Planning

**Step 1 — Human: Setting Intent**
"A B2B campaign for a new SaaS product. Target: IT decision-makers at mid-size companies. Core message: ease of integration with existing systems. Constraint: no direct competitor comparisons."

**Step 2 — AI: Exploration**
AI generates five campaign concepts, each using a different metaphor (bridge, puzzle, plug, conversation, growth).

**Step 3 — Human: Evaluation + Using the Surprise Effect**
- "The puzzle metaphor evokes 'complexity' → rejected"
- "The plug metaphor is surprisingly strong. Why? → It visually conveys technical simplicity"
- "The growth metaphor is too generic → rejected"

Here, a three-second pause: "I expected the 'bridge' metaphor at first—why is 'plug' stronger?"
This question triggers cognitive restructuring.

**Step 4 — Human: Redirection**
"Center it on the plug metaphor, but focus on 'instant operation' rather than 'connection,' and give me three more variations. Keep the tone technical but not intimidating."

**Step 5 — Final Selection + Human Verification**
- Does this message reflect the target's actual concerns?
- Does it violate the no-competitor-comparison constraint, even indirectly?
- Can I put my name on this and send it out?

### 4-3. Metacognitive Verification Checklist

Every time AI output is received, run it through these four questions:

1. **Pause**: Before seeing AI's first suggestion, what was my hypothesis?
2. **Trace the reasoning**: What is the basis on which AI produced this answer? (What cue did it pick up from the prompt?)
3. **Search for counterexamples**: In what situation could this suggestion be wrong?
4. **Apply to context**: Does this work when placed in the actual field?

These four steps are a practical translation of the metacognitive intervention Lim proposes [3]. The key is step 2. The habit of first asking "why did this come out" before judging AI's answer as "right/wrong" is the starting point of cognitive amplification.

### 4-4. Multi-Perspective Review

Relying on a single AI transfers that model's bias directly into the output. Ways to prevent this:

- **Role separation**: Ask the same AI to evaluate "from an optimistic perspective" and "from a critical perspective" separately.
- **Model crossing**: Where possible, compare answers from different AI models.
- **Human arbitration**: The human always makes the final judgment. Observe the debate among AIs, but decide the conclusion using human intent and value criteria.

The purpose of this structure is not to distrust AI. It is to structurally prevent blind trust in a single source.

## Limitations

Most current research in this field remains at the level of conceptual frameworks and design principles. Di Santi's amplification/delegation distinction [1], Gaggioli's extended creativity [2], Lim's metacognitive intervention [3], and Mei and Weber's thinking-augmentation design [4] all point in a direction, but large-scale empirical validation is still lacking. This article shares that limitation. The workflow and checklist presented here are practical proposals derived from theoretical frames, not optimal solutions proven through experiment. The surprise effect, too, is a working-hypothesis label this article has attached, not a measured psychological phenomenon.

## 5. Conclusion

Let's return to the question raised at the outset. Did generative AI automatically raise creativity? We can now rewrite the question itself. Creativity was never, to begin with, a matter of total generation volume. Whether support, synergy, or symbiosis, the deeper the relationship grows, the more the task left to the human converges on a single thing: who holds the cycle of selection—setting intent, interpreting output, and redirecting again. For the person who holds that cycle, AI becomes an amplifier that broadens thought; for the person who loses that cycle, it becomes a delegation sink that swallows judgment whole.

Even with the research still at an early stage, one thing is clear. In the AI era, the bottleneck of creativity is not generative capacity. Generation is already overflowing. The bottleneck is **the quality of selection**—what to choose, why to choose it, and how to redirect again. The ability to design this selection structure becomes the core competency on the human side of human-AI collaboration.
