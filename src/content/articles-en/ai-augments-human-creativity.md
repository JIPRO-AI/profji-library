## AI and Creativity: Designing Better Selection Structures, Not More Generation

Generative AI writes text, creates images, and codes. Has creativity automatically increased? No. Even if AI spews out 100 variations, the output becomes noise if humans cannot decide what to choose or why to choose it.

This article makes one core argument. **The key to AI enhancing creativity is not more generation, but humans designing better selection structures.** Here, a selection structure refers to the cycle of setting intent, evaluating value, verifying, and re-prompting. When this structure works, AI becomes a tool for Cognitive Amplification. When it's absent, AI becomes a trap of Cognitive Delegation.

This distinction stems from the measurement framework proposed by Eduardo Di Santi [1]. Di Santi offers a conceptual framework for distinguishing whether humans experience an expansion of their cognitive abilities (amplification) or an outsourcing of them (delegation) when interacting with AI. While this framework has not yet undergone large-scale empirical validation, it provides the most useful starting point for designing human-AI creative collaboration.

## 1. Extended Creativity: A Collaboration Structure, Not a Tool

Andrea Gaggioli and colleagues organize the human-AI relationship into three stages through the conceptual framework of 'Extended Creativity': support, synergy, and symbiosis [2]. The core insight of this framework is that for AI to enhance creativity, the relational structure itself between human and AI must change, not simply "generate more."

In the support stage, AI performs tasks like data retrieval, draft generation, and variation suggestion. In the synergy stage, human input and AI output interact iteratively, refining ideas. In the symbiosis stage, the boundary between human and AI roles becomes fluid, and their combination produces results neither could reach alone.

The key point is that as the stage ascends, the human role does not shrink but **changes**. In the support stage, the human decides "what to ask for." In the synergy stage, they judge "how to interpret this output and change direction." In the symbiosis stage, they evaluate "does this outcome reflect my intent and values?" At each stage, the human's cognitive contribution rises to a higher level.

What happens if this structure is ignored? Throwing "write 20 marketing copies" at AI and using one verbatim falls short of even the support stage. The human did not set intent, evaluate the output, or re-prompt. This is precisely the cognitive delegation Di Santi warns about [1].

## 2. The Surprise Effect: The Moment AI Actually Changes Human Thinking

The most interesting psychological phenomenon in human-AI collaboration is the 'Surprise Effect.' When AI produces an unexpected output, a crack forms in the existing cognitive structure. This crack becomes a catalyst for opening new thinking pathways.

It works like this. A designer requests a "minimalist brand logo," and AI produces a concept combining African traditional patterns with Bauhaus geometry. The designer is taken aback. But within that surprise, a cognitive reconstruction occurs: "Why did it produce this?" → "Is this combination possible?" → "Was I defining minimalism too narrowly?" This is the surprise effect.

This effect is important because it increases Cognitive Flexibility—the ability to switch thinking strategies to fit new situations. Humans have a strong tendency to rely on past experiences and familiar patterns. AI's unpredictable outputs forcibly break this pattern dependence.

However, for the surprise effect to lead to creativity, a condition must be met. The human must **go through the process of interpreting and judging** that surprise. Blindly accepting AI's odd output is uncritical acceptance, not a surprise effect. Conversely, immediately rejecting it loses a learning opportunity. The key is pausing for 3 seconds to think "why did it produce this?" These 3 seconds are the fork between cognitive amplification and cognitive delegation.

## 3. The Trap of Cognitive Delegation: When Convenience Erodes Ability

As AI grows more powerful, two traps become easier for humans to fall into.

**Anchoring Effect**: The phenomenon where AI's first suggested answer becomes the reference point for all subsequent judgments. A marketing planner sees AI's first suggestion and then evaluates all other options only by "how different are they from the first one?" Chaeyeon Lim suggests design directions for metacognition-based intervention strategies to mitigate such bias [3]. The core idea is to create a structure where users first formulate their own hypothesis before receiving AI output, then compare it with the AI's output.

**Automation Bias**: The tendency to automatically trust AI's answers. The moment "because AI said so" becomes the basis for judgment, human critical thinking ability begins to atrophy. Katelyn Xiaoying Mei and Nic Weber distinguish two design directions for AI to augment critical thinking [4]. One supplements thinking the human has already performed (Performed). The other grows human thinking ability itself by AI demonstrating examples of good thinking (Demonstrated). This distinction remains at the level of design principles, but suggests an important direction: when building AI systems, they should be designed as "partners that show how to think," not "tools that give answers."

Yun Dai emphasizes that student Agency is a key variable for creativity in AI-assisted learning environments [5]. Here, agency is not mere control, but the ability to receive external stimuli, interpret them oneself, and assign meaning. When collaborating with AI, if this agency is maintained, cognitive amplification occurs. If agency disappears, it degenerates into cognitive delegation.

In summary, the core risk of cognitive delegation is the gradual erosion of ability. A single delegation is convenient. But repeated, judgment ability in that domain weakens like "muscle loss" [1]. This is precisely why deliberate friction must be designed into real-world workflows.

## 4. Practical Workflows: Turning Abstract Frames into Operational Models

That's the theory. Now, how to apply it in practice.

### 4-1. Role Division Matrix

| Stage | Human Role | AI Role |
|------|------------|----------|
| Intent Setting | Define goals, constraints, success criteria | — |
| Exploration | — | Generate diverse variations (minimum 5) |
| Evaluation | Check for bias, judge value, review contextual fit | — |
| Re-prompting | Modify direction, add new constraints | Regenerate reflecting modified conditions |
| Final Decision | Selection + Responsibility | — |

The core of this matrix is that **there is no stage where AI completes the task alone.** Even in the exploration stage, the human first specifies "along which axes to secure diversity."

### 4-2. Concrete Workflow Example: Marketing Campaign Planning

**Stage 1 — Human: Intent Setting**
"Campaign for a new SaaS product, B2B. Target: mid-sized enterprise IT decision-makers. Core message: ease of integration with existing systems. Constraint: no direct competitor comparison."

**Stage 2 — AI: Exploration**
AI generates 5 campaign concepts. Each uses a different metaphor (bridge, puzzle, plug, dialogue, growth).

**Stage 3 — Human: Evaluation + Utilizing Surprise Effect**
- "Puzzle metaphor evokes 'complexity' → Reject"
- "Plug metaphor is unexpectedly strong. Why? → Visually conveys technical simplicity"
- "Growth metaphor is too generic → Reject"

Here, pause for 3 seconds: "I initially expected a 'bridge' metaphor, but why is plug stronger?"
This question triggers cognitive reconstruction.

**Stage 4 — Human: Re-prompting**
"Focus on the plug metaphor, but with emphasis on 'immediate operation' over 'connection.' Create 3 more variations. Tone: technical but not intimidating."

**Stage 5 — Final Selection + Human Verification**
- Does this message reflect the target's actual concerns?
- Does it not violate, even indirectly, the 'no competitor comparison' constraint?
- Can I put my name on it and release it?

### 4-3. Metacognitive Verification Checklist

Whenever receiving AI output, run through these 4 questions:

1. **Pause**: Before seeing AI's first suggestion, what was my hypothesis?
2. **Trace Reasoning**: What is the basis for AI giving this answer? (What clues did it pick up from the prompt?)
3. **Search for Counterexamples**: In what situation could this suggestion be wrong?
4. **Contextualize**: Will this work when placed in the actual field?

These 4 steps are a practical translation of the metacognitive intervention Lim proposed [3]. The core is step 2. The habit of asking "why did this come out?" before judging AI's answer as "right/wrong" is the starting point for cognitive amplification.

### 4-4. Multi-Perspective Review

Relying on a single AI imports that model's biases directly. Prevention methods:

- **Role Separation**: Ask the same AI to "evaluate from an optimistic perspective" and "evaluate from a critical perspective" separately.
- **Cross-Model Comparison**: If possible, compare answers from different AI models.
- **Human Arbiter**: The final judgment must be made by a human. Observe debates between AIs, but conclusions are decided by human intent and value standards.

The purpose of this structure is not to distrust AI. It is to structurally prevent blind trust in a single source.

## 5. Conclusion

This article summarizes four core concepts.

- **Extended Creativity**: As the relationship with AI evolves from support → synergy → symbiosis, the human role does not shrink but rises to a higher level [2].
- **Cognitive Amplification vs. Cognitive Delegation**: When humans lead intent, evaluation, and re-prompting, it's amplification. When they hand judgment to AI, it's delegation. This boundary is a design problem, not a technology problem [1].
- **Surprise Effect**: AI's unpredictable outputs become a catalyst for breaking human fixed thinking patterns. However, it converts to creativity only when humans interpret and judge that surprise.
- **Metacognitive Verification**: The 4-step process of Pause → Trace Reasoning → Search for Counterexamples → Contextualize is a practical mechanism to prevent cognitive delegation [3][4].

Current research in this field mostly remains at the level of conceptual frameworks and design principles. Di Santi's amplification/delegation distinction [1], Gaggioli's extended creativity [2], Lim's metacognitive intervention [3], Mei and Weber's thinking augmentation design [4] all point directions, but large-scale empirical validation is still lacking. This article shares that limitation. The workflows and checklists presented here are practical proposals derived from theoretical frames, not experimentally proven optimal solutions.

Nevertheless, one thing is clear. In the AI era, the bottleneck for creativity is not generation capability. Generation is already overflowing. The bottleneck is **selection quality**. What to choose, why to choose it, how to re-prompt. The ability to design this selection structure becomes the core competency on the human side in human-AI collaboration.
