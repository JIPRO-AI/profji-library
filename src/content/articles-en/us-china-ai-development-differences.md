Many articles summarize the US-China AI race like this: "China is state-led, closed, and censorship-centered. The US is market-based, open, and open-source-centered. Therefore the US has the long-term advantage." This frame is clean. And it captures only half of reality.

In reality, both countries are evolving into hybrid models that are neither "purely open" nor "purely closed." The US maintains an open innovation ecosystem while simultaneously tightening strategic controls such as chip export restrictions and stricter investment screening. China remains politically controlling while pursuing a technology-diffusion strategy that distributes open-weight models like Alibaba's Qwen and DeepSeek globally.

This article's core argument is this: the direction of AI development is not determined by system ideology alone. What matters more is how compute acquisition, talent attraction, regulatory design, data accessibility, and open-weight distribution strategy combine. This piece analyzes, in order, resource mobilization structures, data and regulation, the open-source paradox, and the military-civilian boundary — weighing the strengths and weaknesses on both sides.

## Resource Mobilization Structure: Concentration vs. Dispersion, and Their Costs

China and the US mobilize AI resources through structurally different models. China runs a state-led, concentrated investment model. The government has designated AI a top priority for national security and economic growth, and deploys capital, infrastructure, and personnel centrally. This approach's strength is speed: once a specific goal is set, resources can be concentrated quickly.

The US runs a market-based, distributed competition model. Private companies such as Nvidia, OpenAI, Google, and Meta lead, with government playing a supporting role. This approach's strength is diversity: multiple companies competing in different directions widens the technological frontier.

But this doesn't simply end at "China is fast, the US is diverse."

The scale of US private AI investment still exceeds China's. As of 2024, the number of major AI models released by US institutions also outnumbers China's [5]. Yet China is closing the performance gap quickly — the gap between US and Chinese models on major benchmarks narrowed noticeably from 2023 to 2024 [5]. In other words, the US still holds the lead, but ignoring the narrowing trend distorts the judgment.

Talent acquisition isn't a simple picture either. China has built a structure effective for short-term project execution by recruiting scientists returning from abroad and placing them in government-affiliated research institutes. The US benefits from academic freedom and active talent mobility between companies, which favors long-term innovation. But it's also worth considering that US visa policy and geopolitical tensions could negatively affect the inflow of foreign talent.

## Data and Regulation: Both Sides Mix Control and Openness

Data accessibility and regulatory frameworks are also hard to split into black and white.

China's data environment has two sides. On one side is vast data from a population of 1.4 billion and government-led construction of large-scale datasets. On the other, political censorship can cause data on certain topics to be omitted or distorted. Both forces operate at once. "Data is abundant" and "data is biased" are not a contradiction but a coexisting reality.

The US data environment isn't a cure-all either. Information access is relatively free, but problems exist: privacy protection regulation, copyright litigation, and data monopolies. Regulatory capture — where US AI companies exert broad influence over regulatory debate — has also been reported [2]. This means corporate interest does not always align with the public interest.

Worth noting in the regulatory framework: China's regulation doesn't reduce to simple "censorship." China implemented Interim Measures for generative AI in 2023, and in 2025 additionally brought into force labeling rules for AI-generated content and related national standards. This is a structure that combines "control + industrial cultivation + public-service discipline." Within this structure, political censorship does, of course, limit the scope of technical experimentation. But summarizing it as "just censorship" misses the complexity of the actual regulatory structure.

The US regulatory environment isn't a straight line either. There is criticism that the rapid spread of generative AI has outpaced the development of ethical guidelines, opening a gap between principle and practice in research settings [3]. And as case studies of ethical AI debates at OpenAI show, approaches to safety, transparency, and public trust vary by actor even within the US [4]. In short, it is hard to say the US holds an ethically consistent frame.

## Redefining the Open-Source Paradox: Where Is the Real Paradox?

Here we reach this article's central point. In the US-China AI race, open source is the most interesting variable. But handling this variable accurately requires distinguishing terms first.

"Open" in AI has three layers. First, open-source means releasing the code, training data, and even the training process. Second, open-weight means releasing the trained model weights while keeping the training data or process private. Third, closed/proprietary means providing only an API, with the model's internals kept private. What is spreading most actively in the AI industry right now is the open-weight model. Meta's Llama, Alibaba's Qwen, and DeepSeek belong to this category.

The existing frame said this: "The US is strong in open source and China is closed, so it's weak in open source." This may have been roughly true through 2023. But as of 2025-2026, it's hard to sustain this single proposition.

In fact, China's open-weight model ecosystem is diverse and growing fast. Alibaba's Qwen series is widely used by developers around the world, and DeepSeek is drawing attention for its reasoning performance. Chinese government support plays a role in this trend as well. In other words, a politically controlling system is selectively leveraging open-weight in its technology-distribution strategy to expand its global influence.

Here is the real paradox. It is not the old "open vs. closed" paradox. The real paradox is this: the contradiction that a politically closed system uses a partial-openness strategy as a means of technology diffusion. And the contradiction that a politically open system is strengthening strategic technology controls.

The US is no different. Traditionally a stronghold of open ecosystems, it is nonetheless trying to block technology leakage through chip export controls and tighter investment screening. In early 2026, the US BIS revised its license-review policy for some high-performance chips destined for China, saying it would review some chips case-by-case. In other words, this is not a total unilateral blockade but a movement mixing negotiation, exceptions, and readjustment.

Comparing the two countries' strategic structures across four axes reveals the substance of this hybrid form.

| Axis | United States | China |
|------|------|------|
| Open/closed strategy | Open ecosystem + strategic export controls (chips, investment screening) | Political control + selective open-weight distribution (Qwen, DeepSeek) |
| Compute & resource mobilization | Private-led distributed competition, largest-scale private investment | State-led concentrated investment, goal-oriented speed |
| Data | Free access + privacy/copyright constraints | Massive scale + bias from political censorship |
| Regulation | Distributed debate + corporate influence (regulatory-capture concerns) | Combination of control and cultivation via interim measures, labeling standards |

Neither column contains a "purely open" or "purely closed" case.

Discussion of how the spread of open-weight/open-source models reconstructs the obligations around AI system safety matters in this context [1]. Releasing model weights raises transparency and accessibility, but it also raises the potential for misuse. How to strike this balance is a challenge for both countries.

## Military-Civilian Divide: Neither Side Is Clean

The military-civilian boundary doesn't split into black and white either.

China's "military-civil fusion" policy creates a structure in which civilian technology can be diverted to military purposes. This is true. But simplifying this into "all Chinese civilian AI leads directly to military use" distorts reality. Even within China, tension exists between the commercial goals of civilian AI companies and the government's security goals.

The US civilian-military boundary isn't always clean either. There is a structure, through bodies like the Defense Innovation Unit, for putting civilian technology to use in defense, and major Big Tech companies participate in defense contracts. AI's dual-use nature — the same technology usable on both civilian and military sides — applies to both countries.

Frankly speaking, in this area it is difficult to precisely compare the two countries' military-civilian boundaries using public data alone. A meaningful comparison would require going into actual procurement structures, technology-transfer pathways, and the real state of enforcement of regulatory documents. So this piece only notes the direction: both countries are expanding the military use of AI, and in the process both face the problem of balancing it against civilian innovation.

## Conclusion: In a Multivariable Game, There Is No Single-Variable Answer

Attempts to predict the direction of the US-China AI race with a single variable are attractive but dangerous.

Neither "the US wins because it has open source" nor "China catches up because its state mobilization is strong" captures the full picture. Reality is a multivariable game.

This article's analysis narrows to one line: China is catching up through the speed of concentrated investment, and the US defends its lead through the diversity of distributed competition, but on none of the four axes — resources, data, regulation, openness strategy — is either country a pure type. The real contradiction lies in a two-way paradox: a politically closed system uses selective openness in distribution, while a politically open system strengthens strategic control.

Where AI development heads depends on how compute acquisition, talent attraction and retention, flexibility in regulatory design, the breadth of data accessibility, and open-weight distribution strategy combine. System ideology is one factor that influences these variables — not the sole determinant.

Three tasks stand out for future work. First, quantitative research tracking the performance gap between US and Chinese AI models over time is needed. Second, empirical analysis is needed on how China's open-weight strategy is actually reshaping global patterns of technology dependence. Third, a clear-eyed assessment is needed of the real effect of chip export controls — whether they are achieving the intended preservation of the technology gap, or instead accelerating workarounds and indigenous development.

The most dangerous move on this topic is to fix a conclusion favorable to one's own system first and fit the evidence to it afterward. Reality does not neatly favor either side.
