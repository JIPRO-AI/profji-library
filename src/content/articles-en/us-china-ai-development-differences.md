Many summaries of the US-China AI race go like this: "China is state-led, closed, and censorship-centric. The US is market-based, open, and open-source-centric. Therefore, the US has the long-term advantage." This frame is clean. And it reflects only half of reality.

In practice, both nations are evolving into hybrids—neither purely open nor purely closed. The US maintains an open innovation ecosystem while strengthening strategic controls like chip export restrictions and investment screening. China is politically controlled, yet deploys open-weight models like Alibaba's Qwen or DeepSeek globally, pursuing a technology diffusion strategy.

This article's core argument is this: The direction of AI development is not determined by a single system ideology. What matters more is how compute acquisition, talent attraction, regulatory design, data accessibility, and open-weight distribution strategies combine. This article analyzes resource mobilization structures, data and regulation, the open-source paradox, and the military-civilian divide in sequence, balancing the strengths and weaknesses of both sides.

## Resource Mobilization Structure: Concentration vs. Dispersion, and Their Costs

China and the US have structurally different AI resource mobilization models. China employs a state-led, concentrated investment model. The government designates AI as a top national security and economic growth priority, deploying capital, infrastructure, and talent centrally. The strength of this approach is speed. Once a specific goal is set, resources can be concentrated rapidly.

The US uses a market-based, distributed competition model. Private companies like Nvidia, OpenAI, Google, and Meta lead, with the government playing a supporting role. The strength of this approach is diversity. Multiple companies compete in different directions, broadening the technological spectrum.

But it doesn't end with the simple "China is fast, the US is diverse."

US private AI investment still exceeds China's. As of 2024, the number of major AI models released by US institutions also surpasses China's. However, China is rapidly closing the performance gap. The difference between US and Chinese models on major benchmarks has narrowed noticeably from 2023 to 2024. In other words, while the US maintains its lead, ignoring the trend of a shrinking gap distorts judgment.

The talent equation is also not simple. China has built an effective structure for short-term project execution through recruiting overseas-returning scientists and placing them in government-affiliated research institutes. The US benefits from academic freedom and active talent mobility between companies, favoring long-term innovation. But it's also necessary to consider that US visa policies or geopolitical tensions could negatively impact the inflow of foreign talent.

## Data and Regulation: Both Sides Mix Control and Openness

Data accessibility and regulatory frameworks are also difficult to split into black and white.

China's data environment has two sides. On one side, there is vast data from a 1.4 billion population and government-led large-scale dataset construction. On the other, political censorship can lead to omission or distortion of data on certain topics. These two forces operate simultaneously. "Abundant data" and "biased data" are not contradictions but coexisting realities.

The US data environment is not a panacea either. While information access is relatively free, problems exist: privacy regulations, copyright lawsuits, and data monopolies. Furthermore, regulatory capture—where US AI companies exert broad influence over regulatory discussions—has been observed [2]. This means corporate interests do not always align with the public interest.

In regulatory frameworks, note that China's regulations are not simply summarized as 'censorship.' China implemented interim measures for generative AI in 2023 and added AI-generated content labeling rules and related national standards in 2025. This is a combined structure of 'control + industrial nurturing + public service governance.' It is true that within this structure, political censorship limits the scope of technical experimentation. But summarizing it as "just censorship" misses the complexity of the actual regulatory structure.

The US regulatory environment is not a straight line either. There are observations that the rapid spread of generative AI has outpaced the development of ethical guidelines, creating a gap between principle and practice in research settings [3]. Also, as seen in case studies of OpenAI's ethical AI discussions, approaches to safety, transparency, and public trust vary significantly among actors within the US [4]. In short, it's difficult to see the US as having a consistent ethical framework.

## Redefining the Open-Source Paradox: Where is the Real Paradox?

Here we reach the article's central point. In the US-China AI race, open-source is the most interesting variable. But to handle this variable accurately, we must first distinguish terminology.

'Open' in AI has three layers. First, **open-source** means publishing code, training data, and the training process. Second, **open-weight** means publishing trained model weights but keeping training data or process private. Third, **closed/proprietary** means providing only an API, with the model internals kept private. Currently, the most actively spreading category in the AI industry is open-weight models. Meta's Llama, Alibaba's Qwen, and DeepSeek belong here.

The old frame said this: "The US is strong in open-source; China is closed and weak in open-source." This may have been somewhat true until 2023. But as of 2025-2026, it's difficult to push this single proposition.

In reality, China's open-weight model ecosystem is diverse and growing rapidly. Alibaba's Qwen series is widely used by developers worldwide, and DeepSeek is gaining attention for its reasoning performance. Chinese government support also plays a role in this trend. In short, a politically controlled system is selectively utilizing open-weight in its technology distribution strategy to expand global influence.

Here lies the real paradox. It's not the old "open vs. closed" paradox. The real paradox is this contradiction: a politically closed system is using a partially open strategy as a means of technology diffusion. And the contradiction that a politically open system is strengthening strategic technology controls.

The US is similar. Traditionally a champion of open ecosystems, it also seeks to block technology leakage through chip export controls and strengthened investment screening. In early 2026, the US BIS modified its license review policy for some high-performance chips for export to China, stating some chips would be reviewed case-by-case. In other words, it's not a complete unilateral blockade but a movement mixed with negotiation, exceptions, and readjustment.

Discussions on how the spread of open-weight/open-source models reconstructs obligations for AI system safety are important in this context [1]. Publishing model weights increases transparency and accessibility but also raises the potential for misuse. How to strike this balance is a challenge for both nations.

## Military-Civilian Divide: Neither Side is Clean

The military-civilian divide is also not black and white.

China's 'civil-military integration' policy creates a structure where civilian technology can be diverted for military purposes. This is true. But simplifying that all Chinese civilian AI is directly connected to military use distorts reality. Tensions exist within China between the commercial goals of civilian AI companies and the government's security objectives.

The US civilian-military divide is not always clean either. Structures like the Defense Innovation Unit leverage civilian technology for national defense, and major Big Tech companies participate in defense contracts. The **dual-use** nature of AI—the same technology can be used for both civilian and military purposes—applies to both countries.

Frankly, in this domain, precise comparison of the military-civilian boundaries of both nations based on public data alone is difficult. Meaningful comparison requires examining actual procurement structures, technology transfer pathways, and enforcement of regulatory documents. Therefore, we only note the direction here. Both nations are expanding the military application of AI and face the problem of balancing it with civilian innovation.

## Conclusion: In a Multivariable Game, There is No Single-Variable Answer

Attempts to predict the direction of the US-China AI race with one variable are attractive but dangerous.

"Open-source exists, so the US wins" or "State mobilization is strong, so China catches up" do not capture the full picture. Reality is a multivariable game.

Summarizing the analysis in this article: In resource mobilization, China's strength is the speed of concentrated investment, while the US's is the diversity of distributed competition. But ignoring China's speed in closing the performance gap distorts judgment. In data and regulation, both nations have hybrid structures of control and openness; they cannot be summarized as "purely open" or "purely censored." The core of the open-source paradox lies in the bidirectional contradiction: a politically closed system uses selective openness in technology distribution, while a politically open system strengthens strategic technology controls. The military-civilian divide is not clean for either side, and the dual-use challenge is universal.

The direction of AI development depends on how compute acquisition, talent attraction and retention, regulatory design flexibility, the breadth of data accessibility, and open-weight distribution strategies combine. System ideology is one factor influencing these variables, not the sole determinant.

We propose three future tasks. First, quantitative research tracking the performance gap between US and Chinese AI models over time is needed. Second, empirical analysis is needed on how China's open-weight strategy is actually changing global technology dependency patterns. Third, a sober assessment is needed of the actual effects of chip export controls—whether the intended technology gap is being maintained, or if circumvention and indigenous development are being accelerated.

The most dangerous thing in this topic is first placing a conclusion favorable to one's own system and then fitting the evidence to it. Reality does not neatly favor either side.
