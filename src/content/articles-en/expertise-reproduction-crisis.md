## Introduction: The More Perfect the Automation, the More Vulnerable the Human

As the level of automation increases, so does the required intervention capability of the operator. Yet the operator does nothing while the automation runs. Therefore, when intervention is truly needed, that capability has already degraded. This is the paradox presented by industrial engineer Lisanne Bainbridge in "Ironies of Automation" in 1983 [1]. Published over 40 years ago, this paradox is now being reenacted more precisely and across a wider range—in medicine, law, finance, and education—in the form of AI.

But this time, it's not merely a problem of skill degradation. Something more fundamental is being dismantled. The structure that forms experts—the very human mechanism by which expertise is formed, transmitted, and renewed—is quietly collapsing alongside the proliferation of AI-assisted environments.

This article makes one core argument: AI does not automate the *output* of experts; it erodes the *structure* that forms, maintains, and transmits expertise. The result is a crisis of expertise reproduction. And this crisis shares the exact same structure as Bainbridge's irony: it becomes more severe the more accurate the AI becomes.

## What Is Expertise Reproduction? First Defining by What It Is Not

To properly discuss expertise reproduction, we must first clear away commonly confused concepts.

Expertise reproduction is not AI storing or replicating expert knowledge. AI generating a doctor's diagnostic report, drafting a lawyer's contract, or summarizing an analyst's report does not constitute expertise reproduction. This is merely replicating the *output* of expertise. Output and capability are different.

Expertise reproduction is also not the maintenance of certification or licensing systems. Institutional certification is an exit gate for expertise, not the process of its formation itself. The existence of a license does not mean the path to acquiring it is functionally operational.

**Expertise reproduction is the process by which judgment criteria, tacit knowledge, responsibility structures, and creative problem-solving abilities are continuously formed, transmitted, and renewed over time within individuals and organizations.** This is the core of the five-stage model of expertise proposed by Dreyfus and Dreyfus [7]: Experts (stages 4-5) are not people who search for rules; they are people whose judgment operates intuitively the moment they see a situation. This intuition is formed only through the cycle of repeated experience, direct performance that carries the possibility of failure, and correction based on that performance. This transition does not occur from reading in a classroom or checking AI output.

The table below contrasts common misconceptions about expertise reproduction with its actual meaning.

| Aspect | Common Misconception | Actual Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| What is expertise reproduction? | AI storing/replicating expert knowledge | The process where judgment criteria, tacit knowledge, and responsibility are formed and transmitted within humans |
| What is an expert? | Someone who knows a lot of information | Someone whose judgment criteria operate intuitively according to context (Dreyfus 5-stage model) |
| How is expertise transmitted? | Knowledge transfer | The repeated apprenticeship cycle of draft-feedback-judgment |
| The real danger of AI automation? | Firing experts | The environment that creates experts disappearing |
| What is the expertise crisis? | AI being more accurate than experts | Humans losing the capacity to verify AI's judgment |

## The Three-Stage Weakening of the Reproduction Structure by AI Automation

These are not three parallel problems. One begets the next in a causal chain.

### Stage 1: Superficial Compression of Knowledge

AI systems learn patterns from vast case studies but do not internalize the causal structure of those patterns. The problem is this doesn't apply only to AI. Once humans start relying on the patterns provided by AI, they too begin to converge on superficial processing.

The research by Sparrow, Liu, and Wegner (2011) empirically demonstrated this mechanism at the cognitive level [5]. The mere fact that information can be obtained anytime via search engines lowers the motivation to memorize that information. Humans adjust their cognitive strategy to remember only *where* knowledge is, rather than internalizing the knowledge itself. The same mechanism operates in AI-assisted environments. Experts distribute cognitive load by referencing AI output rather than engaging in deep reasoning.

As Bainbridge pointed out [1], this distribution is not harmless. While automation operates, deep expert human capability goes unused, and unused capability degrades. Output may appear to be produced as before, but the capacity to critically review that output gradually becomes shallower.

As this pattern referencing becomes routine, the human role is naturally repositioned from the agent generating judgment to the reviewer approving results.

### Stage 2: Degradation from Judgment Agent to Formal Reviewer

Superficial compression redefines the human role itself. The expert is gradually degraded from an agent generating judgment to a reviewer approving the judgment generated by AI.

Parasuraman and Manzey (2010) named this phenomenon automation bias and automation complacency [2]. When a role becomes specialized in repeatedly reviewing the options presented by an automated system, the ability to detect errors the automation missed—options not presented—structurally declines. This is not a matter of will. It's because the allocation of attentional resources is dictated by the role structure.

It is precisely at this point that the core mechanism of expertise reproduction—the **apprenticeship cycle**—is severed. In traditional expertise formation, trainees directly write drafts, receive feedback from seniors, and internalize those judgment criteria, repeating this process hundreds of times. The act of writing a draft itself reveals one's own level of judgment; feedback corrects that level; and the accumulation of corrections forms intuition. This is the essence of apprenticeship.

When AI replaces the draft, the trainee is positioned as a verifier without going through the process of forming judgment criteria. Without writing a draft, one's own judgment level is not revealed; if not revealed, there is no correction; without correction, intuition does not form. The first link of the apprenticeship cycle is broken.

### Stage 3: Atrophy of Creative Reasoning Muscle and Severance of Tacit Knowledge Transmission

The severance of the apprenticeship cycle leads to the atrophy of creative reasoning ability at the individual level and the severance of tacit knowledge transmission at the organizational level.

Risko and Gilbert (2016) clarify the cognitive consequences of cognitive offloading [3]: Repeated acts of delegating cognition to the external environment induce cognitive skill decay, which degrades even already-formed abilities. If external resources disappear, internal capabilities disappear with them. In AI-assisted environments, this mechanism operates more systematically: When AI intervenes routinely, the expert's cognitive resources gradually converge only onto residual areas not handled by AI. This is because offloading becomes structurally fixed, not selectively applied.

According to the Dreyfus and Dreyfus five-stage model [7], expert-level intuition is formed only through repeated direct performance in situations that can fail and through contextual correction experiences. In an environment where AI preemptively blocks errors and suggests optimal paths, this formation process itself vanishes. Trainees are classified as "proficient" having seen only the results without experiencing the path. But that proficiency is a proficiency stuck at stages 2-3 (rule-referencing level) of the Dreyfus model.

The mixed-methods literature review by Natali, Marconi, et al. (2025) systematically analyzes this in the medical field [6]. In radiology, when the AI reference rate exceeds 70-80%, diagnostic confidence begins to depend on algorithm verification, and competency decline appears across physical examination, differential diagnosis, clinical judgment, and patient communication. More serious is "upskilling inhibition"—the phenomenon where the very path to training specialists is blocked.

The same structure is observed in domains beyond medicine. In law, concerns are raised that since AI contract review tools became widespread, new lawyers have fewer opportunities to accumulate experience in independently identifying non-standard clauses or unprecedented risk conditions. As long as AI handles standard patterns, what newcomers face are only exceptional cases. But the judgment to handle exceptions is built not from handling exceptions, but from directly handling standards hundreds of times.

The field of colonoscopy quantifies this decline. According to a multicenter observational study at four Polish endoscopy centers [8], when endoscopists experienced with AI assistance switched to procedures without AI, their adenoma detection rate significantly dropped from 28.4% to 22.4%. When returning to an environment without AI, the independent judgment capability that had atrophied from disuse was revealed in numbers.

## Trust Collapse Is an Effect, Not a Cause

When discussing the AI expertise crisis, the most frequently proposed prescription is "Explainable AI (XAI)." The logic is that making the black box transparent will restore trust. But this prescription misdiagnoses trust collapse as the cause.

The research by Lee and See (2004) redefines human-automation trust as 'calibrated trust' [4]. The core is this: Only when trust corresponds precisely to the actual capability of the automated system is 'appropriate reliance' possible. Over-reliance is the state of uncritically following automation even when it's wrong; under-reliance is the state of not utilizing automation even when it's right. Both are results of trust not being calibrated to system capability. They emphasize that trust is not a fixed state formed once, but a dynamic process that must continuously correspond to system performance.

But calibrated trust is only possible with expertise. To discern whether automation's judgment is right or wrong, one must first have the capability to judge independently in that domain. Even if explainable AI displays "malignancy probability 73% for this patient's lung nodule," without the capability to evaluate that number within a clinical context, the explanation is merely a formal tool for covering bases. Explainability is only valid when there is a competent human.

In an environment where the expertise reproduction structure has collapsed, no matter how transparent the AI introduced, there is no capacity itself to utilize that transparency. Trust collapse is not a result of lack of transparency. It is a result of the expertise reproduction crisis. It is the endpoint of the three-stage causal chain.

## Design Principles to Restore the Expertise Reproduction Structure

Below are five operational principles for maintaining the expertise reproduction structure in AI-assisted environments. Three core principles directly protect the expertise formation structure, and two auxiliary principles visualize and inspect that structure. This is not a regressive argument to "use AI less." It is the direction of workflow design to intentionally block this dismantling, under the paradox that the more accurate AI becomes, the faster the reproduction structure disintegrates.

First, the structural differences between traditional expertise formation environments and AI-assisted environments are summarized.

| Traditional Expertise Formation Environment | Risk Structure of AI-Assisted Environment |
|---|---|
| Trainee generates draft → Senior feedback → Internalization of judgment criteria | AI generates draft → Trainee's role reduced to verifier |
| Intuition formation through direct performance and correction experience (Dreyfus stages 4-5) | AI preemptively blocks errors → Direct experience opportunities vanish |
| Explicit attribution of judgment responsibility | Blurred AI-human boundary → Responsibility gap occurs |
| Apprenticeship cycle: Observation → Execution → Feedback → Correction | Observation exists, direct execution opportunities decrease |
| Expert judgment criteria transmitted along with context | Algorithmic output replaces judgment criteria |

### Core Principles: Protect the Expertise Formation Structure

**1. Design workflows so AI cannot replace the draft in the apprenticeship cycle**

Design workflows so AI does not intercept the draft-feedback-judgment apprenticeship cycle. For experts in training, AI should be a tool for cross-checking *after* forming a judgment, not a tool that presents answers *before* judgment. This distinction is the method to preserve the core link of the apprenticeship structure.

- Application condition: Particularly strictly applied to experts in training stages (residents, trainee lawyers, junior analysts, etc.)
- Limitation: Realistic application is limited in high time-pressure environments (emergency medicine, real-time trading, etc.)

**2. Separate outcome automation from judgment assistance**

Position AI output not as the final step of the judgment process, but as reference material. The flow should be: expert first forms an independent judgment, then compares with AI output. The structure where AI presents the conclusion first and the human confirms it is a breeding ground for automation bias.

- Application condition: Prioritized in high-risk judgment domains like medicine, law, safety
- Limitation: Processing speed and efficiency decrease. May conflict with short-term performance metrics, volume-based evaluation

**3. Mandate periodic AI-unassisted environments**

Operate environments where expert judgment is performed without AI assistance at least quarterly. This is not "turning off AI." It is a design to preserve a sufficient level of cognitive desirable difficulty so judgment capability does not atrophy. The more perfectly AI operates, the higher the frequency of this training should be. Bainbridge's irony precisely demands this [1].

- Application condition: Integrated into expert certification processes, training evaluations, periodic competency assessments
- Limitation: Conflicts with short-term performance metrics. When application in real environments is impossible due to reasons like patient safety, simulation-based operation is needed

### Auxiliary Principles: Visualize and Inspect the Structure

**4. Mandate recording the boundary between AI judgment and human judgment**

Ensure that all final outputs explicitly distinguish the contribution of "AI suggestion" and "human judgment." This clarifies responsibility attribution and creates a metacognitive structure that makes experts themselves aware of how much they are actually judging. The record itself serves a self-reflective role.

- Application condition: Start with domains with regulatory environments (medical records, legal documents, financial reports)
- Limitation: Documentation burden increases. Risk of formalization—distinctions are recorded but substantial judgment differences are absent

**5. Mandate trust calibration checks with every AI system change**

Whenever an AI model is updated or replaced, measure and recalibrate the over-reliance/distrust patterns of the expert group. When the system changes, human dependence patterns also change. As Lee and See emphasize [4], trust calibration is not a fixed state but a dynamic process requiring continuous management.

- Application condition: During major version updates of AI systems, model replacements, expansion to new domains
- Limitation: Designing calibration metrics itself requires domain expertise. Relying solely on external tools risks calibration becoming formalized

## Conclusion: To Use AI Well, You First Need Humans Who Can Judge Without It

The solution to the expertise reproduction crisis is not in limiting AI's capabilities. On the contrary, the more accurate AI becomes and the more domains it handles, the more the direction of the solution must be sought on the opposite side.

What we must design is not a system where AI assists experts. It is a system that intentionally maintains the environment where experts are formed, even in the AI era. The cycle of directly writing drafts, experiencing failure, receiving feedback to internalize judgment criteria, and bearing that responsibility under one's own name—without this cycle, experts are not made.

AI suggests it can efficiently shorten this process. But this process must not be shortened. Because this process *is* expertise.

The paradox lies here: The humans who can utilize AI best are those who can judge without AI. If we don't make those humans first, AI will be left to judge alone, with no one even itself able to verify it. And there will be no human to confirm it either.
