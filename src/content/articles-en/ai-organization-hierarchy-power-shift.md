AI does not flatten organizations. It changes the location of decision-making authority. In the past, front-line employees made decisions based on context. Now, a central system generates scores, and front-line employees execute them. Authority hasn't disappeared. It has simply moved from the field into the model.

Consider a local tax office employee. In the past, when selecting delinquent taxpayers, they made comprehensive judgments based on regional characteristics, economic conditions, and family circumstances. Long experience was decision-making ability. Now, AI analyzes thousands of data points to automatically select high-risk delinquents. The algorithm outputs delinquency probability as a percentage, but its calculation process and local context are not considered. The employee no longer has the opportunity to make holistic judgments. Their role is reduced to executing the list presented by the AI, and substantive decision-making authority moves to the central data science team that designs and adjusts the system. This is not a simple change in work tools. It is a shift in the location of power within the organization.

There is an optimistic view that AI technology will flatten and democratize organizational hierarchies. This essay shows the opposite. The effects of AI adoption must be re-read not through the technical frame of 'automation,' but through the frame of organizational power: 'authority transfer.' First, the black-box nature of AI replaces contextual judgment on the front line with standardized outputs, causing authority transfer. Second, this transfer distorts decision-making transparency, blocking the operational layer's ability to provide oversight. Third, as a result, a small group of designers who control code and data emerge as a new power center.

## The Spatial Shift and Decontextualization of Operational Authority

A core phenomenon repeats in AI system adoption. The 'contextual discretion' of the operational layer is replaced by the 'standardized parameters' of the design layer. Fourcade and Gordon identified this pattern in national digital surveillance systems. When the sense-judge-act loop is automated centrally, the judgment authority of field bureaucrats is transferred to data science teams [1]. When algorithms play a decisive role in tax prediction or welfare benefit allocation, practitioners can no longer make flexible decisions considering the specifics of individual cases. Their role is limited to managing and applying algorithmic outputs.

This shift is not simple delegation. It is the 'decontextualization' of execution. In the legal field, contract review AI reduces a lawyer's interpretation and judgment to a specific clause-matching task [4]. The context-reading ability of an experienced lawyer diminishes in importance, while the influence of the technologist who designs the software and codes the rules grows. Smart city platforms are the same. They optimize urban operations data with AI, but channels for direct citizen participation are ignored, and decision logic is trapped in the provider's black box [2]. The operating entity loses the ability to understand the premises and processes of decisions occurring in its own domain.

The key is this. Authority doesn't disappear. It just moves from the field into the algorithm. This movement is packaged under the name of efficiency. But look beneath the packaging, and you'll see that the location of judgment has changed.

This phenomenon can be summarized by level of automation as follows.

| Automation Level | Algorithmic Intervention Depth | Operational Layer's Role | Decision-Making Responsibility | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Assistive Tool** | Suggests results | Final judgment referencing algorithmic output | Operational Layer (including managers) | Diagnostic aid system (reference for doctor's judgment) |
| **Suggestive Automation** | Generates & ranks options | Selects and executes from suggested options | Shared between operational and design layers | Selecting final report from AI-recommended options |
| **Conditional Automation** | Rule-based execution | Monitors and intervenes in rule-violating cases | Primarily Design Layer (rule definers) | Automated loan approval based on credit score |
| **Full Automation** | End-to-end judgment & execution | Executes and monitors system output | Design Layer (model designers) | Delinquent taxpayer selection AI, automatic content filtering |

At the 'Full Automation' stage, the operational layer's role is fixed as a simple executor. When algorithms regulate social behavior with embedded norms, like a behavioral scoring system within a metaverse, users cannot access the scoring process and their autonomy is restricted [3]. This opacity is justified under the name of 'efficiency' [6]. Consequently, the space where 'judgment' is performed moves from the operational field to the domain of the technical group designing the algorithm.

Consider a case from a large retail company. They switched their inventory management system to an AI predictive model. In the past, store managers adjusted inventory directly, referencing sales trends, local events, and weather. Even if a sudden local news report caused a specific product to sell out, they could quickly increase orders. The new system learns national sales data and automatically determines optimal order quantities per store. Efficiency improved, but store managers have no authority to ignore system recommendations and adjust inventory. Their expertise and on-the-ground sense are excluded, and inventory decision authority is attributed to the model set by the headquarters AI team. When a system error causes a stockout, is the responsibility with the store manager who doesn't understand the algorithm, or with the headquarters team that designed the model? The problem is that this question itself is not formalized anywhere in the organization.

## The Formation of a New Technical Elite and Power Reproduction

When the transfer of authority from the operational to the design layer solidifies, the organizational power structure itself is reconfigured. The existing elitism based on rank, seniority, and departmental interests begins to be replaced by a technical elitism based on access to and interpretive power over code and data. The core mechanism is the solidification of information asymmetry.

This phenomenon is not confined to corporate organizations. The pattern of actual authority concentrating with designers repeats even in domains where technology promised decentralization. Web3 and Decentralized Finance (DeFi) championed horizontal governance without intermediaries. Yet actual power concentrates with protocol designers and major governance token holders [6]. Those with technical knowledge and code modification abilities monopolize upgrade authority. Research on Russia's digital surveillance infrastructure (SORM-2) also confirms a pattern where a group of data processing experts forms a new axis of power through information monopoly, weakening the role of existing security bureaucrats [5]. The Digiqueer movement resisting algorithmic governance also problematizes this point. It's because the technical group controlling content filtering logic strips both operators and users of discretion and transparency [7].

Across organizations, politics, and platforms, the pattern is one. The group that designs and maintains the algorithm holds de facto authority not found on the formal org chart. Whether decentralization or efficiency was promised, what actually moves beneath that promise is the location of authority.

The power of the technical elite does not stem solely from technical ability. It secures exclusive control over the organization's core decision logic through the black box of the algorithm. Even if existing managers retain formal authority like personnel or budget rights, the ability to understand and modify the basis of substantive decisions made by the AI system lies with the technical elite. This capability gap becomes clear through the contrast of three principles.

### Principle 1: Object of Control
Existing managers control **people and processes**. AI designers control **data flow and model parameters**, thereby indirectly regulating the behavior of people and processes. If a manager exercises authority by giving instructions to employees, an AI designer pre-determines the behavioral direction of the entire organization by defining what output the algorithm will produce.

### Principle 2: Visibility of Decision-Making
A manager's decisions are made visible through discussion and reporting. They can be challenged or requested for review. The core decisions of an AI designer—feature extraction methods, loss function definition, hyperparameter setting—are hidden inside the model, and their impact is only indirectly revealed through the output. Since the decision itself cannot be seen, it cannot be challenged.

### Principle 3: Means of Power Reproduction
Existing elitism reproduces itself through promotion and reward systems. Technical elitism reproduces itself through **barriers to knowledge entry** (mathematics, statistics, computer science) and **system access rights** (production models, raw data). The former is designed by the organization; the latter, even within that organization, is granted only to specific groups. This means power reproduction now occurs through technical infrastructure, not the HR system.

Consider a case from a financial institution. A loan review practitioner decides approval based on the output of an AI risk score model. Senior managers oversee the model's overall performance metrics. However, the people who can first recognize and correct model bias against specific regions or occupations are the data science team that developed the model. They may have lower ranks than managers, but they exercise 'covert power' by substantially intervening in core risk management decisions. This power is not shown on the formal org chart. They don't need to challenge the decisions of practitioners or managers. Because the model pre-defines those decisions. Exercising power while remaining invisible—that is the most characteristic mode of operation of technical elitism.

## Design Principles for Restoring Discretion

Before the power imbalance solidifies, organizations can make design choices. The key is partially returning the interpretive power over algorithmic outputs to the operational layer and creating formal channels to reflect field experience in the design process.

First, mandating Explainable AI (XAI) interfaces. When AI draws a conclusion, it must provide the rationale in a language the operator can understand. A confidence score alone is insufficient. Practitioners must be able to recognize which features received high weight and which cases were judged similar.

Second, including operational layer representatives in the design review structure. When a model is retrained or key parameters are changed, a formal channel is needed for practitioners to directly assess the impact of those changes on the field and provide feedback. Without this process, design decisions continue to be made detached from field experience.

For these two approaches to be effective, the organization must be able to answer the following questions itself.

- Is there a procedure to challenge AI outputs?
- Can field personnel record and report model errors?
- Is operational layer feedback reflected during model retraining?
- Is there no disadvantage to rejecting an AI recommendation?
- Does the design team regularly review field exception cases?

If the answer to any of these is "no," then authority transfer is already underway. And that transfer was never officially decided in any meeting. Training the operational layer in AI literacy is also a prerequisite. They don't need to fully understand the algorithm, but without the ability to critically read its logic and limitations, any design participation will likely remain at a formal level. You must be able to hear the explanation to challenge it, and you must be able to challenge it for authority to remain alive.

## Discussion of Limitations

The framework discussed so far does not apply identically to all organizations. In large corporate environments where resources and power are sufficiently concentrated, this mechanism operates clearly. However, its intensity and form vary depending on organizational size, AI adoption method, and industry regulatory environment. Three constraints must be noted.

First, in SMEs or resource-limited organizations, a distinct 'design layer' may not form. When external commercial AI services (SaaS) or open-source models are adopted as-is, authority transfer occurs not internally but to the external provider. Instead of an internal technical elite emerging, the entire organization becomes dependent on external designers. The lack of internal design capability merely transfers power concentration externally.

Second, the universalization of open-source large language models (LLMs) and prompt engineering technology has the potential to partially dismantle algorithmic exclusivity. As model architectures and training methodologies are disclosed, the secret domain of design may shrink. However, access to high-quality data, large-scale computing infrastructure, and integration know-how tailored to organizational context still act as new entry barriers. The possibility of technological democratization exists, but it does not automatically lead to power dispersion.

Third, in fields with strong explanation obligations and verification requirements, like finance or healthcare, the black-box nature of AI decision-making itself can become an adoption barrier. If regulators require algorithmic audits or mandate human control over final decisions, the unlimited authority of the design layer is constrained. In this case, the operational layer gains legal grounds to challenge AI outputs, and the design layer bears responsibility for explaining its decision logic externally. However, as Ellis points out, regulation itself may reinforce another technical bureaucracy, merely reconfiguring power transfers among elites [7]. The efficacy of regulation depends on the entities that design and enforce it.

The org chart hasn't changed. Ranks, reporting structures, and meeting formats remain the same. But substantive decision-making authority has already moved. From the discretion of the operational field into the parameters of the algorithm. The language of efficiency and objectivity is used to justify this movement, but that language itself is also a choice of the group designing the algorithm. When we uncritically accept the speed and convenience AI brings, what we give up is not simply work discretion. We lose the very ability to perceive the location of power within the organization. We even lose awareness that we have lost that ability. More precisely, we are made to feel that such awareness is unnecessary—that is the most sophisticated mode of operation of the new technical elitism.
