## Why Are We More Tired When AI Reduced Our Work?

Kim, an associate in the marketing team, used AI well. He finished a 3-hour task in 40 minutes. His team leader noticed. "Kim, you handle this." Data from another team, an executive report, new research. His official title stayed the same, but he became the "team's AI person." Six months later, he quit. The prompts, workflows, and know-how Kim created were never transferred to anyone. Team efficiency dropped to zero.

Efficiency was 10. With AI, it became 20. When that person left, it became 0. **10→20→0.** This is not a one-off team incident. It is a recurring structural pattern in organizations that adopt AI.

Bainbridge (1983) pointed out the irony of automation 40 years ago—as automation advances, the tasks left for humans are exception handling, judgment, and supervision, which are cognitively more expensive than simple tasks [5]. In the AI era, this irony has sharpened. **AI burnout occurs when individual cognitive overload overlaps with organizational structural failure.** Neither alone causes burnout. When both operate simultaneously, key talent leaves and efficiency drops to zero.

## AI Burnout Is Not Work Burnout — Defining the Boundaries

We must first clarify what AI burnout is **not**.

Work burnout, as defined by Maslach and Leiter (2016), occurs when the volume of work is excessive [1]. AI burnout occurs even when the volume of work decreases. Technostress, classified by Salanova et al. (2013), is the burden of learning a new tool [6]. AI burnout hits harder those who are already proficient with the tool. The better you are, the more work flows to you.

| Distinction | Work Burnout | Technostress | AI Burnout |
|------|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Cause** | Excessive workload | Burden of learning new tool | AI output verification + organizational structural load |
| **Who it affects** | Anyone | Technology novices | High AI users |
| **Paradox** | Difficulty due to high workload | Difficulty due to tool complexity | **Difficulty despite reduced workload** |

## Why Are We More Tired When AI Reduced Our Work? — The Shift in Cognitive Cost

When AI takes over simple tasks, one might expect rest to fill that space, but what actually fills it is **high-intensity judgment work** [2]. There are three key mechanisms.

**First, verification is more expensive than generation.** AI drafts a report in 5 minutes. But verifying it requires reading from start to finish while constantly judging: "Is this figure correct?", "Is any logic missing?", "Does this fit our situation?" When writing yourself, verification happens simultaneously. AI output is like reviewing someone else's writing. Every editor knows this fact—reviewing someone else's writing is more tiring than writing yourself. And AI output is fluent. Perfect grammar, clean structure. So errors hide within smooth sentences. **Finding errors in well-written text is cognitively far more expensive than fixing poorly written text.**

**Second, micro-decisions explode.** Writing one report without AI involves 5-6 major decisions. Asking AI? Prompt design, output evaluation, revision instructions, re-evaluation, version selection, final confirmation... Dozens of micro-decisions. According to the ego depletion model shown by Baumeister et al. (1998), decision-making consumes finite cognitive resources, and cumulative depletion sharply reduces judgment quality [3].

**Third, as speed increases, so does assigned work.** When AI increases speed, the organization increases throughput. Reports that were 3 per week become 7 per week. Time saved is filled with additional tasks, not rest. "AI cut my work in half, but the company doubled my workload." One more thing—self-written work has a sense of ownership, "my work." Continuously modifying AI-generated work builds cognitive dissonance: "submitting someone else's work under my name." Work is faster, but there's no sense of accomplishment.

These mechanisms do not operate independently. As expectations rise, outputs to verify increase, and micro-judgments explode while searching for errors in fluent output. It's a mutually reinforcing loop.

## The System Burns Them, Not the Individual — The Problem of Organizational Structure

Individual cognitive fatigue alone does not lead to resignation. What drives resignation is **the structure that concentrates this load on one person.**

**Personification of AI capability.** The ability to use AI well remains a specific individual's capability, not an organizational one. Prompts, workflows, and quality control reside only in one person's mind, undocumented and unshared.

**Implicit role shift.** Titles don't change. Compensation doesn't change. But the actual role becomes "team AI infrastructure." "You're good at this, so handle this too" keeps getting added without recognition or reward.

**AI illiteracy of team leaders.** This is one of the most critical bottlenecks. If a team leader hasn't used AI directly, three things fail—they cannot evaluate performance, cannot spread capability, and cannot detect overload [7]. Without top-down direction, they rely on bottom-up capability, and reliance becomes an exploitative structure.

**Adverse selection.** The better someone is at using AI, the more they work, the faster they burn out, and the faster they leave. Those who remain are those who don't use or can't use AI. This is the organizational version of what Raisch and Krakowski (2021) call the "automation-augmentation paradox" [8]. When that person leaves, prompt templates, automated workflows, and tacit know-how all evaporate. **The single point of failure (SPOF) disappears, and the entire system collapses.**

This is worse in large corporations. Slow decision-making wears down only those running solo on the front lines, rigid rank structures layer the "AI person" role on top of existing duties, and KPIs follow the old system, so even 10x faster work with AI yields no additional reward.

## Why "Let's Review Together" Fails

Many organizations are introducing processes to review AI analysis results alongside practitioner opinions for data-driven decisions—budget allocation, demand forecasting, campaign design, etc. On the surface, it's ideal.

But what actually happens on the ground is this. AI proposes a specific figure. They ask the person in charge for their opinion. The person's calculation: to suggest a different number, I need stronger evidence than AI. Since AI is data-driven, it seems more objective than my intuition. If it's wrong? If I followed AI, it's a "data-driven decision," but if I followed my own judgment and was wrong, it becomes "why did you ignore AI?" **Agreeing with AI is politically safe.**

The anchoring effect proven by Tversky and Kahneman (1974) operates here [4]. If AI proposes a number first, human judgment is pulled toward it. Combined with a liability insurance structure—following AI provides a defense even if it fails, but opposing it and being wrong is personal responsibility. Over time, "review together" changes to "AI decides, people approve." Judgment muscles atrophy.

## Solution Principles — As Sharp as the Diagnosis

### Principle 1: Measure verification labor as a hidden cost

Don't just measure "time saved by AI," also measure **"time spent verifying AI output."** Most organizations measure AI adoption impact only by production time reduction. But the cognitive cost of verification, revision, and final confirmation is captured nowhere. If this hidden cost is concentrated on a specific individual, it's not efficiency but debt. **Limitation:** Quantifying cognitive cost still lacks a standard methodology. Self-report-based tracking is a practical alternative but has accuracy limits.

### Principle 2: Manage high AI users not only as productivity assets but also as risk assets

Employees good at using AI are assets that produce results. Simultaneously, if capability is concentrated in that person, they are also the **organization's single point of failure.** You must see both simultaneously. Documenting and sharing prompt templates, workflows, and verification checklists reduces resignation risk while raising team capability [7]. **Limitation:** AI utilization know-how is close to tacit knowledge, making pure documentation insufficient for transfer. Direct experience transfer like pair work must accompany it.

### Principle 3: Flip the structure where AI answers and people approve

**Current (Problem):** AI provides an answer → people review (essentially a rubber stamp).
**Ideal:** People use AI as a tool to analyze first → AI provides a baseline → people's field insight is layered on top.

The key difference is the position of the agent. In the former, the person is a reactor. In the latter, the person is an interpreter and decision-maker. For this structure to work, both **top-down** (team leader provides direction, tools, guidelines) and **bottom-up** (front lines use AI as a tool in their domain) are needed simultaneously. **Limitation:** Securing the team leader's AI literacy is a prerequisite, and this itself is the hardest change. If the team leader has never used AI, they cannot provide direction, tools, or protection.

### Principle 4: Don't leave AI utilization as 'invisible work' outside KPIs

If the AI utilization role is not included in the official job description, it is goodwill-based additional labor. Goodwill depletes. The role must be included in the job description, reflected in evaluations, and designed into compensation [8]. **Limitation:** Large corporate HR systems struggle with this flexibility, and HR system changes take a long time. But as long as informal roles remain unofficial, the 10→20→0 pattern repeats.

## Where Is Our Organization Now?

| Stage | Characteristics | Risk |
|------|------|------|
| **1. Declarative** | Executive slogans, "AI First" | Front-line non-response |
| **2. Educational** | Team leader workshops, demos | Ends at "heard about it" |
| **3. Interventionist** | Dedicated team directly applies to operations | Dedicated team = new SPOF |
| **4. Structural** | AI integrated into KPIs, roles, evaluation | Risk of bureaucratization |
| **5. Cultural** | AI is a basic tool | Hardest to reach |

To prevent 10→20→0, you must reach at least stage 4 (Structural). **Diagnostic question: If the person who uses AI best in our organization quits tomorrow, what happens?** If the answer is "big trouble," you're still below stage 3.

## What This Framework Does Not Solve

**Industry differences.** This analysis focuses on knowledge work. Manufacturing, logistics, and service industries may require different frameworks. **Lack of empirical data.** AI burnout is an academically under-researched phenomenon. This paper's mechanism analysis is based on adjacent field research (automation irony [5], decision fatigue [3], job burnout [1]) and field observation; direct empirical evidence requires future research. **Individual differences.** Burnout thresholds vary by domain expertise, metacognition level, and stress tolerance. **AI technology advancement.** As output accuracy improves, verification burden may decrease, but "AI you can use without verification" has not been reached yet.

## Efficiency Is Not a Blessing, But a Condition

This article is not an argument "don't use AI." As Davenport and Kirby (2016) presented, in well-designed organizations, complementary division of labor between humans and machines actually increases efficiency and quality simultaneously [7]. The problem is that the premise of a "well-designed organization" is mostly unmet.

> **What you can do tomorrow:**
>
> **Individual:** Don't just count "time saved by AI," also count "time spent verifying AI output." Don't verify all outputs with the same intensity.
>
> **Team Leader:** Use AI directly at least once. Only then will you know what your team member is doing. If work is piling up on the team member who uses AI well, that's not performance but risk.
>
> **Organization:** Can you answer "If the person who uses AI best quits tomorrow?" If the answer is "big trouble," start systematizing now.

Whether AI saves the organization or burns people out first depends not on model performance but on **who bears the human judgment cost and how.**
